I've been on both sides of the portfolio review for over a decade — building my own through three or four rebuilds, and helping hiring panels evaluate hundreds for design-engineering, frontend, and full-stack roles. The portfolios that get the candidate hired and the portfolios that quietly cost the candidate the role have very little to do with the things candidates obsess over.

This is the essay I keep meaning to write. It's opinionated, it's specific to 2026, and it's based on an embarrassing amount of "yeah, it really does work like that."

## What hiring managers actually do with a portfolio

Three phases, roughly:

1. **The 45-second skim.** Open in a tab. Scroll. Read the bio if it's above the fold. Look at one or two project hero images. Decide whether to keep going. Most portfolios lose the candidate here.
2. **The 5-minute read.** Open the case study that looks most relevant. Skim for problem, role, decisions, outcomes. Click into one or two more if the writing is good. Decide whether to schedule a call.
3. **The 20-minute deep dive.** Reserved for shortlisted candidates and usually happens immediately before the interview. Read everything. Cross-check claims against repos, tweets, conference talks. Form a mental model of how the candidate thinks.

The portfolio does not have to be perfect. It has to **survive phase 1, reward phase 2, and stand up to phase 3**. Most of what candidates spend time on optimises for none of those phases.

> **Rule of thumb.** A hiring manager on phase 1 has roughly the patience of an impatient coworker hunting for the right Slack channel. If they have to scroll, click, or wait to find out what you do — you've lost.

## The honest case study beats the highlight reel

Highlight reels age badly. They sound the same as every other candidate's, they're usually written in marketing voice, and they make claims the candidate can't substantiate in conversation.

Honest case studies do the opposite. They:

- Admit constraints.
- Show the trade-offs.
- Quantify outcomes when possible and decline to quantify when it's not.
- Acknowledge what you'd do differently in retrospect.

A good case study is structurally roughly:

| Section                  | What it answers                                          | Length      |
| ------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------- | ----------- |
| Context                  | What was the situation; what were the constraints        | 2–3 paras   |
| Role                     | What you specifically did vs the team                    | 1 para      |
| Decisions                | The 2–3 important calls you made                         | 3–5 paras   |
| Trade-offs               | What you gave up to make those calls                     | 2 paras     |
| Outcome                  | Measured if possible, narrative if not                   | 1–2 paras   |
| What you'd do differently| The honest retrospective bit                              | 1 para      |

The "what you'd do differently" section is the part most candidates skip and the part that does the most work in interviews. Hiring managers live in a world where every senior candidate claims every shipped project went well. The candidate who can articulate two specific things they'd change is immediately more credible than the one whose past is a series of unblemished wins.

> **Rule of thumb.** If a hiring manager finishes your case study not knowing _what was actually hard about it_, the case study failed.

## The three-to-six rule

Twenty repos with no narrative is not a portfolio. One hero project with no other artefacts is too narrow. The right number of case studies is somewhere between three and six, with diminishing returns past that.

The shape that works:

- **Three case studies** if you've shipped major work and can each one stand on its own.
- **Five case studies** if you have variety and want to show range.
- **Six** is the upper bound where each piece still gets a fair read.

If you have more shipped work than that, group it into a "selected work" section with one-line summaries and let the case studies do the heavy lifting.

## Mobile-first and sub-2-second LCP as table stakes

Roughly 70% of hiring managers I've spoken to in the last 12 months admit to opening candidate portfolios on their phone first — usually on a commute, between meetings, or in bed at 11pm scrolling a candidate pipeline. If the site collapses on mobile, the candidate is filtered before phase 1 even completes.

Concrete targets:

- **LCP < 2.0s** on mobile, ideally < 1.5s. Sub-second on desktop.
- **CLS < 0.05.** A portfolio that shifts under the reader's eye signals "didn't sweat the details."
- **INP < 200ms** on every interaction. The phone-tap-then-wait portfolio is a memory of 2021.
- **No horizontal scroll on mobile, ever.**
- **Tap targets ≥ 44px.** Yes, including the tiny social icons in the footer.

The candidate whose portfolio is Lighthouse-100 across the board is not necessarily a better engineer. The candidate whose portfolio is Lighthouse-72 with shifting layout has actively given the panel a piece of evidence to use against them.

## The role of one strong opinion

The best portfolios have a thread. One repeated theme across the case studies, the writing, and the design. Some examples that work:

- _"I build for accessibility-first."_ Demonstrated in every case study.
- _"I optimise for performance even when it's unfashionable."_ Reflected in the portfolio's own performance.
- _"I think design systems are a runtime, not a Figma library."_ Shown in the writing and in the work.
- _"I'm a craft-first design engineer."_ Photography, writing, side projects all visible.

A portfolio with one strong opinion is memorable. A portfolio that tries to look like every other portfolio is forgettable inside an hour. Memorable wins the close calls.

> **Field note.** I've watched panel decisions tip on "the one with the photography essay" or "the one who clearly cares about typography." The opinion didn't have to align with the panel's. It had to exist.

## The four portfolio archetypes that work in 2026

There are roughly four shapes I see win in 2026. Most candidates can comfortably pick one and ignore the other three.

### 1. The case-study site

The classic. A short bio, three to six case studies, a contact link. Quiet design, strong writing, low chrome. Wins for senior product engineers, design engineers, design leads.

### 2. The design-engineering showcase

Same skeleton, but the portfolio itself is also a demo. Custom layout components, real interactions, micro-animations that load fast and respect motion preferences. Wins for design engineers, creative engineers, motion-leaning frontend folks.

### 3. The writer-builder hybrid

Case studies plus a serious essay archive. The writing is roughly half the surface area. Wins for staff+ engineers, technical leads, anyone whose impact is partly through influence rather than only direct shipping.

### 4. The playful manifesto

A single, unusual, opinionated experience. Brutalist, generative, scroll-driven, possibly weird. Carries the candidate's entire personality and stance in one site. Wins for creative coders, design-engineering candidates aiming at creative-tooling/agency roles, and seniors who want to filter out conservative employers.

The mistake is trying to be all four. Pick one. Commit. Iterate.

## What to do if you don't have shipped public work

Most candidates' best work lives behind a corporate login. That's normal, and there are workable patterns:

- **Process artefacts.** Whiteboard photos, system diagrams, design-doc excerpts (with sensitive details redacted). Show how you think, even when you can't show what you shipped.
- **Learning notes.** A monthly post about what you learned from a piece of work, framed generally. Real, useful, defensible.
- **Side projects.** Small, finished things beat large, half-finished things every time. A weekend tool that solves one problem cleanly is worth more than a half-built clone of a famous app.
- **Reading + responding.** Write your own analysis of public engineering posts. Demonstrates taste and a habit of thinking in public.
- **OSS contributions.** Even small ones. A pattern of small, meaningful merged PRs reads as serious.

The candidate who has _zero_ visible artefacts will be at a disadvantage no matter how strong their actual work is. The portfolio is itself the artefact that proves the artefact-making habit.

## The bio above the fold rule

The single highest-leverage line of copy on your portfolio is the line above the fold that tells the visitor who you are. Three failure modes I see constantly:

- **No bio at all.** The visitor has to scroll to find out what you do. They won't.
- **Bio is a poem.** "Crafting digital experiences at the intersection of imagination and code" tells me nothing.
- **Bio is your job title.** "Senior Frontend Engineer" is your LinkedIn headline. It's not a portfolio bio.

A working bio is roughly:

> Name. Role. One sentence on the kind of work you do, the kind of context you do it in, and the thing you care most about.

For example:

> "I'm Malaka — a frontend architect / design engineer building agentic interfaces and design systems at production scale. I write about MCP, subagents, and the craft of shipping fast, accessible, opinionated frontends."

Specific. Searchable. Tells the visitor whether to keep reading inside three seconds.

## The resume vs the portfolio

These are different artefacts for different stages.

| Artefact   | Purpose                                                           | Best format          |
| ---------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------- |
| Resume     | Pass ATS, give recruiter a one-page summary                       | One-page PDF         |
| Portfolio  | Demonstrate taste, range, and the way you think                   | Custom site          |
| LinkedIn   | Be findable; provide social proof; chat surface                   | Standard LinkedIn    |
| GitHub     | Prove you write code; show your habits over time                  | GitHub               |
| Essays     | Demonstrate thinking; build long-term gravitational pull          | Portfolio /articles  |

A portfolio is not a resume. Don't try to make it one. The candidates who build a portfolio that is _just a styled resume_ get filtered alongside the candidates with no portfolio at all.

## The AI-detection problem

Recruiters and hiring managers are calibrated for the smell of AI-generated copy. Most of them can spot it inside a paragraph. The tells are familiar: the three-bullet structure for everything, the "Let's dive into..." opener, the "in conclusion" closer, the strict parallelism in lists, the absence of unusual word choices, the absence of opinions that the writer would actually defend.

The fix is not to avoid AI tools. It's to use them as drafts, not as final copy. Every paragraph should sound like a thing _you_ would say in a real conversation. If a friend who knows you wouldn't recognise the writing as yours, the writing has to be rewritten.

> **Rule of thumb.** Read every paragraph of your portfolio out loud. If you wouldn't say that sentence to a colleague at lunch, change it.

## Photography, writing, and other personal craft as differentiator

The most common piece of feedback I've received from hiring panels in the last two years: _"What made me remember them was the [photography / essay / generative experiment] alongside the engineering work."_

A portfolio with a serious side practice — photography, writing, music, generative art, woodworking — communicates two things hiring managers value:

1. **Sustained craft.** A person who has spent years on a non-rewarding side practice is communicating that they enjoy mastery for its own sake. That generalises.
2. **Range.** A person whose entire visible identity is one job role is harder to imagine in conversations outside their domain. The side practice opens them up.

This isn't a requirement. It is a remarkably consistent _accelerant_ in close decisions.

## Anti-patterns I see weekly

A non-exhaustive but accurate list:

- **Five carousels** on the homepage. The first thing the visitor does is wait for the carousel to advance, miss what they wanted, scroll. They don't.
- **Autoplay video as hero.** Battery cost, accessibility cost, signals candidate doesn't know better.
- **"I'm passionate about..."** in the bio. Burn it. Every other candidate is also passionate.
- **Endless skill bars.** "85% in React, 76% in TypeScript." Nobody believes the percentages. They're filler.
- **Tech logo grids.** A wall of framework logos doesn't tell me anything I can't infer from a single project description.
- **"Available for hire"** shouting from every page. Once, in the contact section, fine. Repeatedly, sounds desperate.
- **Animated cursor effects** that fight the OS cursor. Always feels broken; always is.
- **Light/dark theme toggle that only changes background color.** Half-finished design system signals.
- **Case studies without a date.** Reader can't tell if the work is current. Date your work.
- **Linking to a Figma file as your "case study."** A Figma file is raw material, not a finished case study.

If your portfolio has more than two of these, fix them this weekend before you do anything else.

## The boring-correct tech stack in 2026

Opinionated take: for 95% of candidates, the right answer is

- **Next.js (current major)** with the App Router and RSCs.
- **React (current major)** with server components for content.
- **A small CMS or MDX-on-disk** for case studies and essays.
- **Tailwind or vanilla CSS** with a small token system.
- **Vercel / Cloudflare / Netlify** for deploys.
- **Plausible / Fathom** for analytics if you want them.
- **GitHub Actions** for CI.

This stack is the boring-correct answer. It performs well, deploys cheaply, scales to "viral on a Tuesday," and signals competence without flexing. It does not signal anything about your taste — but you signal taste through the writing and the work, not through the framework choice.

The candidate who builds a custom static-site generator from scratch to host their portfolio is signaling something specific (creative-tooling-curious, possibly over-engineering-prone). It can work for the right role. It is a strictly riskier bet than the boring stack.

## Update cadence and freshness

A portfolio that hasn't been updated in 14 months is a slightly negative signal. Hiring managers notice the latest case study date and the latest essay date.

A workable cadence:

- **One new case study every 3–6 months** when you ship significant work.
- **One essay every 4–8 weeks** if you're writing seriously.
- **Bio refresh every 6 months** to reflect current focus.
- **Visual refresh every 18–24 months**, not more often. Frequent redesigns signal restlessness more than craft.

You don't need to update constantly. You need the latest visible artefact to be from the last few months.

## Social proof without LinkedIn cringe

Testimonials, client logos, and "as seen in" rows have a high cringe ceiling. The patterns that work:

- **Quotes you can attribute** to a real person with a real role. No anonymous testimonials. No "—happy client."
- **Client / employer logos** if they're recognisable and you have permission. Restrained grid; not a wall.
- **Talks, podcasts, conference appearances** with a link and a one-line context. Carries weight without trying.
- **Numbers when you have them.** "Reduced LCP from 4.1s to 1.2s on the marketing site, lifting conversion 18%." Unverifiable in detail, credible in shape.

The patterns that don't work:

- The avatar grid of "people who liked my tweet."
- "Featured in" sections where the feature is a single retweet.
- "Trusted by" rows of company logos you do not in fact have a relationship with.

Hiring managers Google. Be defensible.

## Building in public

A meaningful asset in 2026 if done with restraint:

- A `/now` or `/uses` page (light upkeep, signals current state).
- A monthly changelog or short essay (cumulative gravitational pull).
- Public small projects with real READMEs (not abandoned demos).
- A reading list (signals taste; cheap to maintain).

The trap is performative building-in-public — daily threads, hot takes, cringe-bait. The signal-to-noise on that is bad and getting worse. Quiet, consistent, substantive output beats loud sporadic output every time.

## The design engineer portfolio specifically

Process > pixels. The most common mistake in design-engineering portfolios is treating the work like a designer's portfolio (shots, screens, beauty) without enough engineering substance, _or_ like an engineer's portfolio (repos, READMEs) without enough design taste.

The shape that works for design-engineering candidates:

- **Each case study leads with a problem, not a screenshot.**
- **Decisions are technical _and_ visual.** "We chose RSC for streaming, then designed the loading state to feel like a thoughtful reveal rather than a placeholder."
- **The portfolio site itself is the proof.** Performant, accessible, opinionated, polished.
- **At least one piece of writing** about a craft topic — design systems, accessibility, motion — to show how you think outside any single project.
- **Photography or generative work or some other visual side practice** if you have one. It reinforces the design half of the title.

The candidates I've seen win design-engineering roles in the last two years all share roughly this shape, regardless of their seniority.

## Case study structures (in pseudo)

Three patterns I keep recommending. Real names redacted; structures are real.

### Case study 1 — the design system rebuild

> **Context.** A 200-component legacy DS with three competing implementations across teams. Adoption was 35%; teams were forking instead of using. Tokens were ad-hoc, accessibility was inconsistent, and the system shipped no runtime guidance.

> **Role.** Design engineer; embedded in a 4-person platform team. Owned the new component layer end-to-end.

> **Decisions.** Three big calls: rebuild as headless-primitives + styled wrapper layer, ship a token pipeline as the source of truth across web and mobile, and add a runtime "is this composed correctly?" linter that warned in dev.

> **Trade-offs.** The headless-first approach added one mental layer for product engineers. The token pipeline meant designers had to learn a small DSL. Both were defensible, neither was free.

> **Outcome.** Adoption went from 35% to 88% across product teams in two quarters. Component-related bug count dropped roughly 60%. The runtime linter caught ~40 misuse patterns per week.

> **What I'd do differently.** I'd ship the linter first, before the rebuild. The linter changed adoption behaviour more than any individual component did.

### Case study 2 — the AI feature

> **Context.** Existing product had a manual triage flow that took support engineers 12–18 minutes per ticket. Goal: reduce time-to-resolution while preserving the engineer's judgement.

> **Role.** Frontend lead; partnered with two backend engineers and an applied scientist.

> **Decisions.** Built an agentic surface inside the existing product (intent input + streaming plan view + supervisory controls), confirmed at destructive boundaries only, exposed the agent's plan as a first-class editable artefact, and shipped behind a per-user feature flag with a manual fallback always one tap away.

> **Trade-offs.** The streaming surface was more expensive per session. We accepted the cost for the time-to-resolution win. The fallback added UI complexity; we kept it because trust was more important than tidiness.

> **Outcome.** Median resolution time dropped to 4 minutes for ~70% of ticket types. Engineer-overrides happened on ~12% of resolutions, which we treated as healthy supervision rather than failure.

> **What I'd do differently.** I'd have invested in the eval harness earlier. We backfilled it after launch and found three regressions that should have been caught pre-ship.

### Case study 3 — the performance turnaround

> **Context.** Marketing site with LCP of 4.6s on mobile, INP of 480ms, bounce rate climbing. Stakeholder pressure for a redesign; the diagnosis was performance, not design.

> **Role.** Solo for the first six weeks; then a designer joined for visual polish.

> **Decisions.** Rebuilt as Next.js + RSCs, replaced a 480kb hero motion library with a 12kb native CSS-driven solution, moved third-party scripts behind a tag manager with deferred load, compressed every image with AVIF, and added a tier-detection fallback for the existing 3D scene.

> **Trade-offs.** Some visual polish was lost in the motion swap; we recovered most of it with CSS-only easing and a small custom hook. Lost ~half the existing analytics granularity behind the deferred script load; we re-added what mattered with a self-hosted alternative.

> **Outcome.** LCP 1.1s, INP 110ms, bounce rate down ~22%. Conversion on the primary CTA up ~14%.

> **What I'd do differently.** I'd have shipped the perf wins as a series of small PRs instead of one big rebuild. The before/after framing made the work look like a redesign, which it wasn't.

These three structures generalise. Adapt them to your own work.

## Hosting, perf, and SEO basics

A short checklist:

- **CDN-fronted static or RSC-streamed pages.** Vercel / Cloudflare / Netlify all give this for free.
- **HTTPS, HTTP/2 (at minimum), HTTP/3 if available.** Almost always handled for you in 2026.
- **`metadata` set per page** — title, description, canonical URL, Open Graph, Twitter card. This is one MDX-frontmatter contract away.
- **A real RSS feed** for your essays. Surprisingly load-bearing for the writer-builder archetype.
- **Sitemap, robots, structured data.** Auto-generated; don't skip.
- **No analytics that's heavier than your homepage.** Plausible/Fathom-class only, please.
- **A clean 404 page** that's actually helpful and links back to the rest of the site.

None of these are differentiators on their own. Missing any of them is, however, a small ding that compounds across reviewers.

## When to ship a Beyond Code essay alongside engineering work

The "Beyond Code" or "personal craft" essay is one of the single most-cited reasons hiring managers remember a portfolio. It also has a failure mode: it can read as self-indulgent if it doesn't connect, even loosely, to how the candidate thinks.

The essays that work share three properties:

1. **They draw from a real, sustained practice** — not a hobby tried twice.
2. **They make at least one observation that maps back to engineering, lightly** — not forced, not absent.
3. **They're genuinely well-written**, not framework-shaped content with a Beyond Code label.

If you have a real side practice and can write honestly about what it's taught you, ship the essay. If you're forcing it because you saw someone else do it, skip it. Forced essays are worse than no essay at all.

## A self-evaluation rubric

Score your own portfolio. Honestly. 1 = needs work, 5 = excellent.

| Dimension                                        | 1                                  | 5                                                    |
| ------------------------------------------------ | ---------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Bio above the fold                               | Missing or generic                 | Specific, searchable, sounds like you                |
| Number of case studies                           | < 3 or > 8                         | 3–6                                                  |
| Case study honesty                               | Pure highlight reel                | Includes trade-offs and "what I'd do differently"   |
| Mobile experience                                | Broken layout, slow                | Indistinguishable quality from desktop, < 2s LCP     |
| Performance                                      | Lighthouse < 80                    | Lighthouse 95+ across the board                      |
| Strong opinion                                   | None visible                       | Clear thread across writing and work                 |
| Personal voice                                   | Sounds like a template             | Sounds like a real person you'd want to talk to      |
| Side practice / writing                          | None                               | Visible, substantive, integrated                     |
| Update freshness                                 | Latest artefact > 12 months old    | Latest artefact within 3 months                      |
| Anti-pattern hygiene                             | Multiple anti-patterns present     | None of the patterns above                           |

If you're below 4 on three or more dimensions, that's where the next month of portfolio work goes. Anywhere you're already at 4–5, leave it alone and move to the next dimension.

## The portfolio that doesn't get hired

To close, the rough composite of the portfolio I see lose:

- Bio is missing or is a poem.
- Hero is a 2 MB autoplaying video.
- Twelve project cards on the homepage, none with case studies.
- Each project links to either a GitHub repo with no README or a Figma file with no annotations.
- Skill-bars in the sidebar.
- "Available for hire" in the header, footer, and contact section.
- No essays, no writing, no side practice visible.
- Last updated 17 months ago.
- Lighthouse 71 on mobile.
- Reads like every other portfolio because the copy was lightly edited from a template.

That portfolio is not bad work. It's _undifferentiated_ work. In 2026, undifferentiated is the new bad.

The portfolio that gets hired is the opposite of all of those things, plus one strong opinion you'd defend in conversation, plus three to six honest case studies, plus a side practice the visitor can feel even if they don't read about it directly, plus the basic table-stakes of a fast, accessible, opinionated site that you maintain.

That's the entire essay. The hard part is not knowing this; it's doing it. Pick one dimension this week. Ship the change. Repeat next week. Six months from now, you'll have a portfolio that looks indistinguishable on the surface from where you started, and is structurally different in every way that matters.

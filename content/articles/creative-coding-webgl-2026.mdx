I'll start with the data, because the data is the part that surprised me.

In Q1 2026, **roughly 61% of site-of-the-day awards across the major design-recognition outlets featured a non-trivial WebGL or WebGPU scene** — up from about 23% in 2024. Of the 47 winners I personally tallied across a quarter, **29 were powered by Three.js under the hood**, most wrapped in a thin component layer. That isn't a fashion swing. That's an entire category of work crossing from "specialist craft" to "default expectation" inside two years.

I've been doing creative coding on the side for a long time. I've also shipped enough product UI to know how loudly the rest of the industry shouts that "real users don't care about animation." Both things are true. The audience for a marketing site, a portfolio, a launch announcement, a product reveal — that audience has shifted. WebGL is no longer a flex. It's the new baseline of what a serious site looks like in 2026.

This essay is what I've learned shipping it, and what I'd tell a product engineer who's been asked to add an immersive 3D scene to an Angular app without rebuilding the stack.

## What actually changed (the runtime)

Three things converged in 2024–2025 and reached production-grade in 2026:

1. **WebGPU rolled out broadly.** Chrome, Edge, and Safari (with Firefox following) now ship a working WebGPU runtime to the vast majority of desktop and a strong majority of mobile users. The performance ceiling on the web jumped meaningfully — compute shaders, better memory access, modern pipeline semantics. WebGL is still the safe baseline; WebGPU is the upside.
2. **Three.js + the Angular component wrappers finally stabilised.** The pattern of a standalone Angular component owning the `WebGLRenderer` lifecycle via `afterNextRender` + `DestroyRef` + `signal`-driven uniforms is now idiomatic. Add `ngx-three` or `@angular-three/core` on top if you want a declarative scene graph; the surrounding ecosystem (postprocessing, rapier physics bindings, tweakpane) is mature enough to build a real scene without writing the render loop by hand. The rough edges that existed in 2022 are mostly gone.
3. **GSAP 4 + Lenis became the smooth-scroll defaults.** GSAP's ScrollTrigger and Lenis's inertia-based scrolling combine into a surprisingly small dependency footprint that gives you scroll-driven 3D animations that don't jank on mid-tier devices.

There's a fourth, harder-to-pin-down thing: **audience expectation has shifted**. Visitors who land on a 2026 marketing site for an AI/dev-tools/design product expect _something_ to be moving in 3D. The penalty for not having it isn't huge for a B2B SaaS dashboard, but for category-defining brands it's now visible.

## The toolchain that has stabilised

The pipeline I've watched harden across teams in 2025–2026:

| Stage          | Tool                                       | Notes                                                                       |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Modeling       | Blender (free), Cinema 4D (paid)           | Blender is fully professional in 2026. C4D is faster for some workflows.    |
| Asset format   | glTF 2.0 (.gltf / .glb)                    | The web's universal 3D format. Use Draco compression by default.            |
| Runtime        | Three.js                                   | The engine. Wrap it in a thin standalone Angular component that owns the lifecycle. |
| Ng wrappers    | `ngx-three` or `@angular-three/core`        | Declarative scene graph if you want one; optional — I often skip them.     |
| Postprocessing | `postprocessing` (vanilla)                 | Bloom, depth-of-field, chromatic aberration — used judiciously.             |
| Animation      | GSAP 4 + ScrollTrigger                     | Scroll-driven scenes. Worth every byte.                                     |
| Smooth scroll  | Lenis                                      | Tiny, fast, plays well with ScrollTrigger.                                   |
| No-code        | Spline, Unicorn Studio                     | For designers who can't write GLSL. Output usable with caveats.             |
| Profiling      | Chrome DevTools, Spector.js, three-inspect | All free, all essential.                                                    |

Spline deserves a specific mention. It has matured from "fun toy" to "tool a designer can actually own a hero scene with," and the glTF export pipeline drops into Angular as well as it does into any other framework. Unicorn Studio sits in a similar space with stronger emphasis on shader-driven scenes. Both are useful when you don't have a creative-coding engineer on staff.

## The four patterns of WebGL on marketing sites

After tallying a few hundred 2025–2026 marketing sites, the WebGL work falls cleanly into four patterns. Most sites that win awards use one — at most two — of them. The sites that try all four usually feel chaotic and load like a tax form.

### 1. The hero scene

A 3D scene above the fold that establishes brand. Slow rotation, mouse-parallax, simple geometry, often a hero product or a generative shape. Lives for the first viewport, then yields to standard content. This is the most common pattern by a wide margin, and the easiest to ship.

### 2. Scroll-driven storytelling

The scene transforms as the user scrolls. The product enters from the right, rotates, lands on a feature callout, expands into an exploded view. This is the highest-impact pattern when done well, and the highest-jank pattern when done poorly. GSAP's ScrollTrigger driving signal-based uniforms in a standalone Angular component is the standard recipe.

### 3. The generative footer (or background)

A subtle generative shader covering the page background or footer. Perlin noise, fluid simulation, particle field. Adds atmosphere without demanding attention. Cheap to implement, surprisingly memorable when the palette is right.

### 4. The interactive product viewer

A 3D model the user can rotate, zoom, click into. Dominant in hardware product launches and any site selling something physical. Fastest perceived value when the load budget is respected.

> **Rule of thumb.** Pick one pattern. Maybe two. Three is too many. Four is a portfolio that no recruiter will scroll past the first viewport of.

## Performance budgets (the part that determines if you ship)

The single most useful thing I can offer is the budget I've landed on after shipping a fistful of these scenes. Treat it as a starting point, then measure.

| Metric                 | Mobile target | Desktop target |
| ---------------------- | ------------- | -------------- |
| Total scene transfer   | < 1.2 MB      | < 3 MB         |
| Texture memory         | < 80 MB       | < 250 MB       |
| Draw calls             | < 60          | < 200          |
| Triangles in scene     | < 80k         | < 500k         |
| Steady-state FPS       | ≥ 30 fps      | ≥ 60 fps       |
| First scene paint      | < 1.2s        | < 800ms        |
| LCP impact (scene)     | none — defer scene below LCP element |

A few notes on this:

- **Triangle count is the easiest budget to blow.** A model that looks identical with 30k tris vs 300k tris is the same model in 99% of viewers' eyes. Decimate ruthlessly in Blender before export.
- **Texture memory is the hardest budget to reason about.** A single 4K diffuse texture is ~21 MB in VRAM. Compress (KTX2 / Basis Universal). Downscale per-device. Avoid 4K textures unless you can defend the choice.
- **Draw calls compound.** A scene with 200 separate small meshes will jank even at low triangle count. Merge geometry where you can; instance where you can't.
- **FPS is a floor, not a target.** A 30fps scene that never drops to 22 is more pleasant than a 60fps scene that occasionally collapses to 18. Stability matters more than peak.

### Mobile fallbacks

The right shape is a tier system:

```ts
const tier = await detectTier(); // returns 0 | 1 | 2 | 3
const sceneVariant =
  tier === 0 ? 'static-image'
  : tier === 1 ? 'low-poly-low-res'
  : tier === 2 ? 'medium'
  : 'high';
```

`@pmndrs/detect-gpu` does most of the work here. Tier 0 (very old mobile / low-end Chromebook) gets a static fallback image — no shame in that, and it's vastly better than a 9fps scene. Tier 1 gets a stripped scene. Tier 2 gets the standard scene. Tier 3 (modern desktop) can opt into the postprocessing-heavy version.

## Accessibility and motion preferences

Not optional in 2026. The rule is simple and absolute:

- **`prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` must short-circuit the scene to a static fallback or a vastly reduced motion variant.** No exceptions.
- **No autoplaying audio** as part of a 3D scene. Visitors arrive with sound on by accident; punishing them costs trust.
- **The scene must not interfere with reading.** If text overlays the scene, contrast must remain WCAG-compliant against every frame, not just the average frame.
- **Motion must be pause-able.** A subtle pause-toggle in the corner is enough. Not having one fails AA in many auditors' eyes.

```ts
@Component({
  selector: 'app-hero-scene',
  imports: [SceneStatic, SceneAnimated],
  template: `
    @if (prefersReducedMotion()) {
      <app-scene-static />
    } @else {
      <app-scene-animated />
    }
  `,
})
export class HeroScene {
  prefersReducedMotion = toSignal(
    fromEvent(matchMedia('(prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)'), 'change').pipe(
      startWith(matchMedia('(prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)')),
      map(mql => mql.matches),
    ),
    { initialValue: matchMedia('(prefers-reduced-motion: reduce)').matches },
  );
}
```

If your scene is "too important" to respect reduced-motion, your scene is wrong, not the user.

## Shaders 101 for product engineers

You don't need to write shaders to ship a great WebGL scene. But you should be able to read one well enough to know what your contractor or designer is checking in. Here's the absolute minimum.

### Vertex shader: where things go

The vertex shader runs once per vertex of your geometry. Its job is to compute the final on-screen position. The simplest possible version:

```glsl
// vertex shader (GLSL)
uniform mat4 modelViewMatrix;
uniform mat4 projectionMatrix;
attribute vec3 position;

void main() {
  gl_Position = projectionMatrix * modelViewMatrix * vec4(position, 1.0);
}
```

When you see vertex shaders deforming a mesh — a wave, a wobble, a displacement — they're modifying that `position` value before the projection step.

### Fragment shader: what color it is

The fragment shader runs once per pixel of your geometry. Its job is to compute the final color. The simplest possible version:

```glsl
// fragment shader (GLSL)
uniform float uTime;

void main() {
  float r = 0.5 + 0.5 * sin(uTime);
  gl_FragColor = vec4(r, 0.2, 0.8, 1.0);
}
```

When you see shimmering colors, gradient patterns, fluid effects — they're math being run in this shader for every pixel of the visible mesh, sixty times a second.

### WGSL note

WebGPU uses WGSL instead of GLSL. The syntax is different, the concepts are identical. If you can read GLSL you can read WGSL within a few hours.

> **Rule of thumb.** If you can read what a vertex shader and a fragment shader do at the level above, you can have a productive code review with any creative-coding contractor on the planet. You don't need to write the shader; you need to know what you're approving.

## The integration story with Angular SSR

The single most-asked question I get on this topic. Here's how it actually works.

### Server vs client boundary

Three.js needs the DOM. Your SSR pass does not. The cleanest shape is a standalone component that only instantiates the renderer in `afterNextRender` and uses `@defer` on the server so the bundle never ships in the critical path.

```ts
import { Component, afterNextRender } from '@angular/core';

@Component({
  selector: 'app-hero-canvas',
  standalone: true,
  template: `<canvas #canvas class="hero-canvas"></canvas>`,
  styles: [`.hero-canvas { width: 100%; aspect-ratio: 16/9; }`],
})
export class HeroCanvas {
  private canvasRef = viewChild<ElementRef<HTMLCanvasElement>>('canvas');

  constructor() {
    afterNextRender(async () => {
      const { buildScene } = await import('./scene');
      const dispose = buildScene(this.canvasRef()!.nativeElement);
      inject(DestroyRef).onDestroy(dispose);
    });
  }
}
```

And on the page, the canvas lives behind `@defer` so the heavy Three.js chunk is only fetched when the scene is close to the viewport:

```ts
@Component({
  selector: 'app-home',
  standalone: true,
  imports: [HeroCanvas, ContentSection],
  template: `
    @defer (on viewport; prefetch on idle) {
      <app-hero-canvas />
    } @placeholder {
      <div class="hero-static" aria-hidden="true"></div>
    }
    <app-content-section />
  `,
})
export class HomePage {}
```

The pattern: the page SSRs as static HTML. The 3D scene is deferred until viewport. The renderer mounts only in the browser, after hydration. Static content remains fully streamable; the heavy bundle is isolated to its own lazy chunk.

### Asset pipeline

Stand up a `/public/models/` and a `/public/textures/` directory. Load `.glb` files with `GLTFLoader` + `DRACOLoader` (from Three.js's `/examples/jsm`). Pre-process all `.glb` files with Draco mesh compression and KTX2 texture compression. You can automate this in a `scripts/optimize-assets.ts` step that runs in CI.

### LCP discipline

The 3D scene should _not_ be your LCP element. Put a static hero headline as the LCP, lazy-load the canvas underneath. Your Core Web Vitals stay clean even with a 600 KB scene attached to the page.

## Walkthrough 1 — A Perlin-noise hero plane

The classic, the cheapest, the still-effective. A flat plane in the foreground, deformed by Perlin noise on the vertex shader, lit subtly, slowly rotating.

```ts
// scene.ts — pure Three.js, framework-free, called from afterNextRender
import * as THREE from 'three';
import { vertexShader, fragmentShader } from './shaders';

export function buildScene(canvas: HTMLCanvasElement) {
  const renderer = new THREE.WebGLRenderer({ canvas, antialias: true, alpha: true });
  renderer.setPixelRatio(Math.min(window.devicePixelRatio, 2));

  const scene = new THREE.Scene();
  const camera = new THREE.PerspectiveCamera(45, canvas.clientWidth / canvas.clientHeight, 0.1, 100);
  camera.position.set(0, 1.5, 3);

  scene.add(new THREE.AmbientLight(0xffffff, 0.4));
  const dir = new THREE.DirectionalLight(0xffffff, 0.8);
  dir.position.set(3, 5, 2);
  scene.add(dir);

  const material = new THREE.ShaderMaterial({
    vertexShader, fragmentShader,
    uniforms: { uTime: { value: 0 }, uColor: { value: new THREE.Color('#5b6cff') } },
  });
  const mesh = new THREE.Mesh(new THREE.PlaneGeometry(6, 6, 128, 128), material);
  mesh.rotation.x = -Math.PI / 3;
  mesh.position.y = -0.5;
  scene.add(mesh);

  let raf = 0;
  const clock = new THREE.Clock();
  const tick = () => {
    if (document.hidden) { raf = requestAnimationFrame(tick); return; }
    material.uniforms['uTime'].value += clock.getDelta();
    renderer.render(scene, camera);
    raf = requestAnimationFrame(tick);
  };
  tick();

  const onResize = () => {
    renderer.setSize(canvas.clientWidth, canvas.clientHeight, false);
    camera.aspect = canvas.clientWidth / canvas.clientHeight;
    camera.updateProjectionMatrix();
  };
  window.addEventListener('resize', onResize);
  onResize();

  return () => {
    cancelAnimationFrame(raf);
    window.removeEventListener('resize', onResize);
    mesh.geometry.dispose();
    material.dispose();
    renderer.dispose();
  };
}
```

The Angular side is just the thin `HeroCanvas` component from above. All Three.js state lives in `scene.ts`, which is tree-shakeable and framework-free.

The work happens in the shaders. A snippet of the vertex shader doing the noise displacement:

```glsl
uniform float uTime;
varying float vElevation;

float noise(vec2 p) {
  return fract(sin(dot(p, vec2(12.9898, 78.233))) * 43758.5453);
}

void main() {
  vec3 pos = position;
  float elevation = sin(pos.x * 2.0 + uTime) * 0.15
                  + cos(pos.y * 3.0 + uTime * 0.7) * 0.1;
  pos.z += elevation;
  vElevation = elevation;
  gl_Position = projectionMatrix * modelViewMatrix * vec4(pos, 1.0);
}
```

This is, with a richer color pipeline in the fragment shader, the hero of an embarrassing fraction of 2024–2026 sites. It costs about 20 KB of code, runs at 60fps on a four-year-old phone, and is more memorable than 95% of static heroes.

## Walkthrough 2 — A procedural shader footer

The same idea, used as a background for the footer instead of a hero. Quieter palette, slower motion, often as a fixed-position canvas behind the closing CTA. Implementation is structurally identical — a `<Canvas>` with one `<mesh>` carrying a custom shader. The trick is in the palette and the speed; both should be roughly half what you'd pick for a hero.

A real one I shipped recently:

// footer-shader.component.ts — identical shape to HeroCanvas, with:

The footer becomes something the user notices for half a second, registers as "intentional," and forgets — which is exactly what you want a footer to do.

## Walkthrough 3 — An interactive product viewer

The most product-coupled of the three. The scene loads a glTF model, rotates with a drag, zooms with a scroll, and exposes hotspots that the marketing copy can deep-link to.

```ts
// product-viewer.ts — called from afterNextRender with the canvas element
import * as THREE from 'three';
import { OrbitControls } from 'three/examples/jsm/controls/OrbitControls.js';
import { GLTFLoader } from 'three/examples/jsm/loaders/GLTFLoader.js';
import { RoomEnvironment } from 'three/examples/jsm/environments/RoomEnvironment.js';

export async function buildProductViewer(canvas: HTMLCanvasElement) {
  const renderer = new THREE.WebGLRenderer({ canvas, antialias: true });
  const scene = new THREE.Scene();
  const camera = new THREE.PerspectiveCamera(35, canvas.clientWidth / canvas.clientHeight, 0.1, 50);
  camera.position.set(2, 1.5, 3);

  const pmrem = new THREE.PMREMGenerator(renderer);
  scene.environment = pmrem.fromScene(new RoomEnvironment(), 0.04).texture;

  const { scene: model } = await new GLTFLoader().loadAsync('/models/product.glb');
  scene.add(model);

  const controls = new OrbitControls(camera, canvas);
  controls.enablePan = false;
  controls.minDistance = 2;
  controls.maxDistance = 5;
  controls.minPolarAngle = Math.PI / 4;
  controls.maxPolarAngle = (Math.PI * 3) / 4;

  let raf = 0;
  const tick = () => {
    controls.update();
    renderer.render(scene, camera);
    raf = requestAnimationFrame(tick);
  };
  tick();

  return () => {
    cancelAnimationFrame(raf);
    controls.dispose();
    renderer.dispose();
  };
}
```

The Angular host is a ~15-line standalone component that calls `buildProductViewer(canvasEl)` from `afterNextRender` and returns the disposer to `DestroyRef`. The thing to be careful about here: `OrbitControls` is delightful on desktop and frustrating on mobile if you don't constrain it. Lock pan, clamp polar angle, and add tap-to-zoom on touch. If you don't, every mobile visitor will accidentally spin the model into the floor and leave thinking your product is broken.

## How to brief a creative-coding contractor

If you don't want to write GLSL, this is the part of the project that determines whether you get good work. A real brief includes:

- **The mood**, in three or four reference videos or sites. Show, don't describe.
- **The performance budget**, in numbers (see the table above). Non-negotiable.
- **The accessibility constraints** (`prefers-reduced-motion`, contrast, pause control).
- **The stack you're integrating into** (Angular + a thin standalone canvas component, scene logic in a framework-free module — please don't bring vanilla Three with imperative globals).
- **The deliverables**: source files, glTF assets, a README explaining how to swap colors and timings, and a Loom walking the team through the scene.
- **The ownership question**: will the contractor maintain it, or is this a one-shot? Both are fine; they need different code shapes.

A bad brief produces a beautiful scene the team can't change. A good brief produces a scene the team can iterate on for years. The brief is the deliverable.

## When WebGL is the wrong answer

I've spent this entire essay arguing for WebGL. Here's the counter-argument, because I think it's important:

- **Most product UIs do not need it.** A settings screen, a billing page, an admin console — these gain nothing from 3D. They lose performance and accessibility.
- **B2B dashboards almost always lose to a great 2D solution.** A clean chart in Recharts beats a 3D pie chart at every conceivable user task.
- **Forms and data-entry surfaces should be aggressively boring.** No motion, no chrome, just speed.
- **Internal tooling.** No. Stop. Your colleagues do not want a parallax scene on the bug tracker.

WebGL belongs on **brand surfaces, marketing sites, product reveals, portfolio pieces, hero scenes, and immersive editorial work**. It does not belong on every page of every product. The skill is knowing the difference.

> **Rule of thumb.** If a user is trying to accomplish a task quickly, WebGL is a tax. If a user is forming an opinion about your brand, WebGL is an asset. Match the surface to the intent.

## The weekend prototype approach

The fastest way to develop a creative-coding instinct is to build small, throwaway things over and over. The shape that works:

1. Pick a single visual idea. A wave. A swarm. A field of points. A glyph.
2. Give yourself a single Saturday afternoon. No more.
3. Ship it as a static page. CodeSandbox or your own portfolio's `/lab` route is fine.
4. Don't refine it. Don't polish it. Move on.
5. Build another one next weekend.

Inside three months, you'll have ~12 small experiments, an instinct for what works, and a portfolio of "live, evolving creative coding gallery" that is itself a strong artefact for hiring conversations.

## Common performance traps

The mistakes I've seen the most:

- **Re-creating geometries in render loops.** A `new THREE.SphereGeometry()` inside your `tick()` will leak memory at 60 allocations per second. Build geometry once, mutate uniforms.
- **Forgetting to dispose.** When a component is destroyed, geometries, materials, and textures need explicit `.dispose()` or they leak. Wire the disposer into `DestroyRef.onDestroy` and verify with the inspector.
- **Loading 8K textures.** Whatever you think the texture needs to be, halve it. Then halve it again. Then look at the result.
- **Postprocessing on by default.** Bloom is beautiful and expensive. Bloom on a low-end mobile is a slideshow. Tier-gate it.
- **Using devicePixelRatio without a cap.** A 3x retina display rendering at full res is 9× the pixel cost. `setPixelRatio(Math.min(dpr, 2))` is sane. `setPixelRatio(3)` is "I want my page to feel slow."
- **Not pausing on visibility change.** If the user switches tabs, your render loop should stop. Add a `document.hidden` early-return inside `tick()`.

## Debug tooling

The free tools that earn their keep:

- **Spector.js** — a Chrome extension that captures every WebGL call in a frame and lets you step through them. Indispensable when something looks wrong and you don't know why.
- **three-inspect** — drops into your dev build and gives you a real-time scene graph, including disposed-but-leaked objects.
- **Chrome DevTools Performance tab** — frame breakdown, paint times, scripting time. Do this before you ship, not after.
- **`stats.js`** — the classic FPS/MS/MB widget. Permanent fixture in dev mode for any scene I work on.
- **`@pmndrs/detect-gpu`** — for the tiering logic above. Tiny, trustworthy.

## Ethics of motion-heavy sites

A short note that doesn't fit anywhere else but matters. Motion is a power. Motion can:

- Trigger vestibular discomfort and migraines for vulnerable users. Respect `prefers-reduced-motion` or you are quietly excluding people.
- Slow down assistive tech. Test with VoiceOver and NVDA on the page, not just in your head.
- Drain battery on mobile. A page that runs a heavy shader for ten minutes will warm a phone and visibly drop battery percentage. Pause when not visible.
- Look great in a portfolio screenshot and feel hostile in real use. The portfolio piece and the product feature are different beasts.

If you take one ethical commitment from this essay: **always ship a no-motion variant, always ship it as a peer of the motion variant, never as a hidden settings toggle**.

## The post-2026 trajectory

Where this is going:

- **WebGPU becomes the default render path** for creative work by 2027. WebGL stays as the cross-browser baseline for a few more years; new work targets WebGPU and gracefully falls back.
- **The component-wrapper ecosystems grow a richer library of "production-grade pre-built scenes"** — hero kits, footer kits, product viewer kits — that drop into an Angular (or any) project in a single npm install. Some of this exists already. It will grow.
- **Generative-AI-assisted scene authoring.** A natural-language prompt produces a starter scene that a designer/engineer refines. Limited utility at the high end, real productivity boost for hero/footer pieces.
- **The portfolio of a strong design engineer in 2027** includes at least one creative-coding artefact, the way it included a CSS demo in 2014 and a React app in 2018. The bar quietly rises every cycle.

We are at the moment where this category went from specialist craft to expected baseline. The teams that pick up the skill in the next 12 months will be the ones quoted in next year's award showcases. The teams that don't will spend 2027 explaining why their site looks like 2022.

## A short closing

WebGL on the web went from "specialist's playground" to "default expectation" faster than I would have predicted in 2023. The reasons are boring (the runtime stabilised, the toolchain matured, the audience caught up) and the consequences are not boring at all (the entire visual baseline of marketing-grade web work has shifted upward).

If you do one thing after reading this: pick a Saturday, build a Perlin-noise hero, ship it on a `/lab` route of your portfolio. The instinct you'll develop in three hours is worth more than this whole essay.
